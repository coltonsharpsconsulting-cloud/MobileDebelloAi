Motion Studies · v1

Ten ways to animate a 6×6 grid of data.

A single starting grid — thirty-six rounded cells in six color categories, seven of them carrying notification rings — read ten different ways through motion. Each study triggers on scroll. The point is to compare: same input, different visual argument about grouping, distribution, or intensity.

Starting grid (shared)

01 heat bloom

A center that radiates.

What it represents

A focal point of activity radiating outward — the pattern you get when one region draws disproportionate attention and its surroundings catch the heat. Useful for showing where a user's eye should land first, or where activity concentrates around a hub.

What to look for

Cells near the center intensify and warm; peripheral cells desaturate and shrink slightly. The bloom should feel like heat spreading through metal, not like a hard spotlight. Notification rings should remain readable through the gradient.

02 cluster migration

Like seeks like.

What it represents

Disordered categories self-organizing into contiguous regions. Imagine the same dataset before and after a grouping pass — the colors don't change, only where they sit. Useful for visualizing what clustering algorithms actually do to a layout.

What to look for

Cells physically slide to new positions so same-category cells end up neighboring each other. Movement should feel inevitable, not random. Notification rings travel with their squares — the ring belongs to the cell, not the location.

03 wave propagation

A signal passes through.

What it represents

A signal, alert, or update traveling across the population over time. Notifications cascading from a hub. A status change rippling through dependent systems. Anything where time-of-arrival is the story.

What to look for

A diagonal wave of brightness sweeping from top-left to bottom-right. Each cell brightens, peaks, then settles back. The wavefront should read as one continuous motion, not as thirty-six separate flashes.

04 distribution histogram

Layout collapses into bars.

What it represents

The same 36-cell grid restated as a histogram. Each column becomes one category; bar height encodes how many cells belonged to it. The spatial arrangement gives way to the underlying distribution.

What to look for

Cells outside the bars fade and shrink; cells inside the bars unify in color by column. The transformation should make the distribution legible — you should be able to "read" relative category sizes at a glance once the bars settle.

05 notification ripple

Each alert has a radius.

What it represents

How a single alert reaches and influences its surroundings — proximity-based notification cascades, signal-to-neighbors patterns, blast radius. The notification isn't just a flag on one cell, it's an event that touches what's around it.

What to look for

Concentric rings emanating from every notification cell, in the same color as that cell's static ring. Background cells dim so the ripples have somewhere to sit. Multiple ripples overlap and don't synchronize — they stagger so the grid feels alive.

06 category spotlight

Filter, don't rearrange.

What it represents

Filtering by one attribute while keeping the spatial layout intact. The user isn't asking to re-sort — they're asking "where is X?" The answer should preserve their mental map of the grid.

What to look for

Non-matching cells dim hard but don't move. Matching cells (category A here) stay vivid, scale up slightly, and gain a soft glow that lifts them forward. Their notification rings stay sharp; dimmed cells' rings fade with them.

07 sort to gradient

Chaos resolves into a ramp.

What it represents

A re-ordering of the same data by intensity or value — turning a categorical scatter into a clean monotonic ramp. The shift from "what kind?" to "how much?", visualized in place.

What to look for

Cells smoothly recolor into a gradient from dark to light, top-left to bottom-right. The transition staggers in reading order so it feels like sorting cards into a hand, not like flipping a single switch. Notification rings remain so you can still find the flagged items in the new order.

08 quadrant split

Two axes, four rooms.

What it represents

A 2×2 analysis matrix carved out of the same data — splitting by two binary axes (high/low, active/inactive, urgent/not) to reveal which quadrant each cell belongs to. Useful for prioritization frameworks.

What to look for

The grid cleaves into four 3×3 chunks that drift apart with a visible gutter between them. Each quadrant picks up a subtle tint so the four rooms are distinguishable without losing the individual cells. The gap should breathe — wide enough to read as separation, narrow enough that the four still feel like one system.

09 connection mesh

The grid is just where.

What it represents

Relationships running underneath the spatial layout — who talks to whom, what depends on what. The grid layout is incidental; the real structure is the network. Useful when proximity in the grid doesn't equal proximity in the data.

What to look for

Background cells dim. Lines draw between notification cells, stroked in one segment at a time, in the accent color. Endpoints stay bright. The lines should ease in (not snap) and stagger so you can follow which connections appeared first.

10 density stagger

Heat told through timing.

What it represents

How densely categories pack across the grid — a heat map expressed through timing rather than color. Cells with many same-category neighbors pulse early and hard; isolated cells pulse late and soft. The order of pulses reveals the clusters before the colors do.

What to look for

The animation should "read" the grid for you. A high-density patch lights up first as a group; outliers trail behind. The visual story is which cells belong together, even when the static colors give few hints.
