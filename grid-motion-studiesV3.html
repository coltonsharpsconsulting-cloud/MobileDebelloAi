Family 01 · 5 studies
Focus & intensity — "what should I look at?"

Overlays that don't change the grid's shape. They add a layer of attention on top of the layout, directing the eye without rearranging anything.

01heat bloom

A center that radiates.

Question

Where is activity concentrated? Used for showing a hub of attention and the falloff around it.

Look for

Center cells warm and intensify; periphery dims. Heat through metal, not a hard spotlight.

02wave

A signal passes through.

Question

How does change propagate over time? Notification cascades, viral spread, status changes rippling through dependents.

Look for

One diagonal wavefront sweeping top-left to bottom-right. Reads as a single motion, not thirty-six separate flashes.

03category cycle

Filter by one color at a time.

Question

Where do cells of any one category live? Studying spatial distribution per attribute without re-sorting the grid.

Look for

Tabs are the controls. Tap one to spotlight that color; tap again to clear. Only one active at a time.

04density probe

Your cursor becomes the focus.

Question

What's around any given point? An inspector pattern — point at a region to study it, click any cell to read its metadata.

Look for

Hover (or touch-drag) anywhere on the grid to spotlight that area. Click a cell for a popup. Click again to dismiss.

05connection mesh

The real structure is underneath.

Question

Who is related to whom? Timelines, task dependencies, any network where the grid is just where the nodes happen to live.

Look for

Background dims. Lines stroke between notification cells in sequence so you can follow which connections appeared first.

Family 02 · 4 studies
Distribution — "what's the mix?"

Shapes that re-express the data's composition. Same input, different visual argument about what the proportions actually are.

06quadrant split

Two axes, four rooms.

Question

How does the grid divide along two binary axes? Useful for 2×2 prioritization, importance/urgency, before/after × active/inactive.

Look for

Grid cleaves into four 3×3 chunks that drift apart with a visible gutter. Each quadrant picks up its own subtle tint.

07histogram

Layout collapses into bars.

Question

How many of each? Vertical bars, height by count. The classic distribution view, performed in place.

Look for

Each column becomes one category; cells outside the bars fade, cells inside unify in color. Bars grow with a column stagger.

08donut

Proportion curls into a ring.

Question

What share does each category represent? Proportion-first composition view in a compact circular footprint.

Look for

Cells migrate from grid positions to ring positions, each category occupying an arc proportional to its count. Notification rings travel with their cells.

09treemap

Nested rectangles sized by count.

Question

How do categories pack together when sized by their share? For many categories at once where pie slices get too thin to read.

Look for

Cells collect into rectangular blocks per category, each block visually sized by count. Excess cells fade rather than overcrowd a block.

Family 03 · 1 study
Hierarchy — "what matters most?"

Shapes that argue about priority. The position itself encodes importance, not just category.

10funnel

Where do they drop off?

Question

At each stage, how many remain? Conversion funnels, attrition, any sequential process where you care about the loss between steps as much as the final outcome.

Look for

Each row narrows by one cell. Cells that drop off fade outward; survivors compress toward the centerline.

Family 04 · 1 study
Time & sequence — "in what order?"

Shapes that put cells in temporal order. The position is the timeline.

11calendar strip

Each cell becomes a day.

Question

How does activity spread across calendar time? GitHub-contributions style: 7-wide rows where each square is one day.

Look for

Cells reorganize from 6×6 grid into a 7-column layout, reading across as weeks. The footprint widens slightly to accommodate the extra column.

Family 05 · 1 study
Spatial & relational — "how do these sit together?"

Shapes where position itself carries meaning beyond category or count — affinity, gravity, soft grouping.

12constellation

Six gravity wells, six clusters.

Question

How do affinity groups look when freed from grid lines? For soft clusters where membership isn't strictly bounded by row or column.

Look for

Cells of each category gravitate toward an invisible category center. Result is six soft clouds around the grid's middle, with notification rings still attached.
